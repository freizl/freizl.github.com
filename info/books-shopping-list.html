1. English

  1. 1984: 75th Anniversary (by George Orwell)
  2. 21 Lessons for the 21st Century (by Yuval Noah Harari)
  3. A Book of Abstract Algebra (by Charles C Pinter)
    • Exceptionally clear introduction to abstract algebra. Groups, rings, fields explained with intuition. Perfect for self-study.
  4. A Book of Abstract Algebra: Second Edition (Dover Books on Mathematics) (by Charles C Pinter)
  5. A Programmer’s Introduction to Mathematics (by Jeremy Kun)
    • Math concepts (polynomials, linear algebra, calculus) taught through programming. Bridges the gap between math and code.
  6. A World Without Time (by Palle Yourgrau)
    • Explores the friendship between Gödel and Einstein and Gödel’s proof that time travel is possible in certain universes allowed by general relativity.
  7. Algorithmic Puzzles (by Anany Levitin)
    • 150 puzzles organized by algorithmic technique. Develops problem-solving skills through practice.
  8. An Elegant Puzzle (by Will Larson)
    • Systems thinking for engineering management. Team sizing, tech debt, migrations, org design. Highly practical.
  9. An Elegant Puzzle: Systems of Engineering Management (by Will Larson)
  10. An Invitation to Applied Category Theory (by Brendan Fong)
    • Category theory through real-world applications. Seven sketches covering databases, circuits, logic, and more.
  11. An Invitation to Applied Category Theory: Seven Sketches in Compositionality (by Brendan Fong)
  12. Anger: Wisdom for Cooling the Flames (by Thich Nhat Hanh)
  13. Brave New Words, by Sal Khan (from gatesnotes)
  14. Breath: The New Science of a Lost Art (by James Nestor)
  15. Build: An Unorthodox Guide to Making Things Worth Making (by Tony Fadell)
  16. Categories for the Working Mathematician (by Saunders Mac Lane)
    • The original textbook on category theory by its co-creator. Rigorous mathematical foundations.
  17. Chess for Kids (by Michael Basman)
  18. Conceptual Blockbusting: A Guide to Better Ideas, Fifth Edition (by James L. Adams)
  19. Conceptual Mathematics (by F. William Lawvere)
    • Gentlest introduction to category theory. Co-authored by a category theory pioneer. Makes abstract concepts concrete.
  20. Conceptual Mathematics: A First Introduction to Categories (by F. William Lawvere)
  21. Crafting Interpreters (by Robert Nystrom)
    • Build two complete interpreters from scratch—one in Java (tree-walk), one in C (bytecode VM). Best PL implementation book.
  22. Deep Learning (by Goodfellow, Bengio, Courville)
    • The comprehensive textbook on deep learning theory. Covers foundations, CNNs, RNNs, optimization, regularization.
  23. Drawdown: The Most Comprehensive Plan Ever Proposed to Reverse Global Warming (by Paul Hawken)
  24. Escape from Freedom (by Erich Fromm)
  25. Essentialism: The Disciplined Pursuit of Less (by Greg McKeown)
  26. Feynman’s Tips on Physics (by Richard P. Feynman)
    • Problem-solving supplement to the Feynman Lectures. Exercises and Feynman’s approach to physics problems.
  27. Feynman’s Tips on Physics: Reflections, Advice, Insights, Practice - A Problem-Solving Supplement to the Feynman Lectures on Physics (by Richard P. Feynman)
  28. Flights of Fancy: Defying Gravity by Design and Evolution (by Richard Dawkins)
  29. Formal Development of a Network-Centric RTOS: Software Engineering for Reliable Embedded Systems (by Eric Verhulst)
  30. Genesis: Artificial Intelligence, Hope, and the Human Spirit (by Henry A. Kissinger)
  31. Grain Brain (by Perlmutter David)
    • Claims about gluten and brain health. Controversial; many claims not well-supported by research.
  32. Gödel’s Proof (by Ernest Nagel)
    • Accessible explanation of Gödel’s incompleteness theorems. Short, clear, and mathematically honest.
  33. Gödel’s Proof (by Ernest Nagel)
  34. Homo Deus: A Brief History of Tomorrow (by Yuval Noah Harari)
  35. How to Design Programs (by Matthias Felleisen)
    • Systematic program design through iterative refinement. Teaches how to think about programming, not just syntax.
  36. How to Know a Person: The Art of Seeing Others Deeply and Being Deeply Seen (by David Brooks)
  37. I Am a Strange Loop (by Douglas Hofstadter)
    • Hofstadter revisits GEB themes—self-reference, consciousness, and what makes a “self.” Deep dive into the nature of mind.
  38. I Can’t Make This Up: Life Lessons (by Kevin Hart)
  39. Incerto (Box Set) (by Nassim Nicholas Taleb)
    • Complete collection: Fooled by Randomness, Black Swan, Antifragile, Bed of Procrustes. Framework for thinking about uncertainty, risk, and robustness.
  40. Inspired: How to Create Tech Products Customers Love (by Marty Cagan)
  41. Introduction to Algorithms (CLRS), 4th ed (by Cormen, Leiserson, Rivest, Stein)
    • The definitive algorithms textbook. Comprehensive coverage from sorting to NP-completeness. Reference and learning resource.
  42. Klara and the Sun: A GMA Book Club Pick: A novel (by Kazuo Ishiguro)
  43. Masters of Doom (by David Kushner)
    • Story of John Carmack and John Romero building id Software. Technical innovation, creative conflict, and gaming history.
  44. Math with Bad Drawings: Illuminating the Ideas That Shape Our Reality (by Ben Orlin)
  45. Nobel Writers on Writing (by Ottar G. Draugsvold)
  46. Noise: A Flaw in Human Judgment (by Daniel Kahneman)
  47. Numbers Don’t Lie (by Vaclav Smil)
    • 71 short essays using data to explain modern world phenomena—energy, transportation, food, environment. Smil is Bill Gates’ favorite author for good reason.
  48. Oliver Byrne’s Elements of Euclid: The First Six Books with Coloured Diagrams and Symbols (Art Meets Science Edition) (by Art Meets Science)
  49. Parallel Sorting Algorithms (by Selim Akl)
    • Academic treatment of parallel sorting—networks, hypercubes, mesh algorithms. Specialized algorithms text.
  50. Peak (by Anders Ericsson)
    • The original research behind “deliberate practice.” Ericsson debunks the 10,000 hour myth and explains what actually builds expertise.
  51. Practical Cryptography (by Ferguson, Schneier)
    • How to actually implement cryptographic systems correctly. Covers protocols, key management, and common pitfalls. From two of the field’s best.
  52. Readings in Database Systems (by Hellerstein, Stonebraker)
    • Curated collection of foundational database papers with commentary. The “Red Book” of databases.
  53. Real-World Cryptography (by David Wong)
    • Modern practical cryptography—TLS, secure messaging, passwords, post-quantum. More current than Practical Cryptography.
  54. Say Nothing: A True Story of Murder and Memory in Northern Ireland (by Patrick Radden Keefe)
  55. Search Inside Yourself (by Chade-Meng Tan)
    • Google’s mindfulness and emotional intelligence program. Meditation techniques for the workplace.
  56. Software Abstractions (by Daniel Jackson)
    • Introduction to Alloy—a lightweight formal methods tool for modeling and analyzing software designs. Teaches you to find bugs in designs before writing code.
  57. Software Architecture: The Hard Parts (by Neal Ford)
    • Modern trade-off analysis for distributed systems. Covers decomposition, data ownership, contracts. Essential for architects.
  58. Software Engineering at Google (by Winters, Manshreck, Wright)
    • How Google builds software at scale. Code review, testing, documentation, dependency management, large-scale changes.
  59. Specifying Systems: TLA+ (by Leslie Lamport)
    • Learn TLA+ from its creator. Formal specification for concurrent and distributed systems. Used at Amazon, Microsoft.
  60. Summerhill School: A New View of Childhood (by A. S. Neill)
  61. Team Topologies (by Matthew Skelton, Manuel Pais)
    • Framework for organizing engineering teams around cognitive load and flow. Introduces four team types and three interaction modes. Directly applicable to scaling organizations.
  62. The Beginning of Infinity (by David Deutsch)
    • Physicist on the nature of knowledge, progress, and explanations. Optimistic epistemology; problems are solvable.
  63. The Creative Act: A Way of Being (by Rick Rubin)
  64. The Feynman Lectures on Physics (by Feynman)
    • Feynman’s legendary Caltech lectures. Physics taught with unparalleled insight and clarity. Three volumes covering mechanics, electromagnetism, quantum.
  65. The Garbage Collection Handbook (by Richard Jones)
    • Comprehensive reference on automatic memory management. Algorithms, implementations, and trade-offs. Deep systems knowledge.
  66. The Great Influenza (by John M. Barry)
  67. The Hard Thing About Hard Things (by Ben Horowitz)
    • Brutally honest advice on running startups. Covers layoffs, firing, politics, and the struggles no one talks about.
  68. The Hard Thing About Hard Things: Building a Business When There Are No Easy Answers―Straight Talk on the Challenges of Entrepreneurship (by Ben Horowitz)
  69. The Innovator’s Dilemma (by Clayton Christensen)
    • Why great companies fail when disruptive technologies emerge. Sustaining vs disruptive innovation frameworks.
  70. The Juggling Act: Bringing Balance to Your Faith, Family, and Work (by Pat Gelsinger)
  71. The Lambda Calculus: Its Syntax and Semantics (by Henk Barendregt)
    • THE definitive reference on lambda calculus—the theoretical foundation of functional programming. Rigorous and comprehensive; essential for PL theory depth.
  72. The Making of Prince of Persia: Journals 1985-1993 (by Jordan Mechner)
  73. The Miracle of Mindfulness: Gift Edition (by Thich Nhat Hanh)
  74. The ONE Thing: The Surprisingly Simple Truth About Extraordinary Results (by Gary Keller)
  75. The Perfectionists: How Precision Engineers Created the Modern World (by Simon Winchester)
  76. The Scout Mindset (by Julia Galef)
    • How to actually update beliefs based on evidence rather than defending existing positions. Distinguishes “soldier mindset” (defend) from “scout mindset” (seek truth).
  77. Type Theory and Formal Proof (by Rob Nederpelt)
    • Rigorous introduction to type theory and lambda calculus. Foundations for understanding modern PLs and proof assistants.
  78. UNIX: A History and a Memoir (by Brian W Kernighan)
    • First-hand account of Unix development at Bell Labs by one of its creators. Essential computing history.
  79. Willful Blindness: Why We Ignore the Obvious at Our Peril (by Margaret Heffernan)
  80. The Design of Everyday Things (by Don Norman)
  81. My Own Words by Ruth Bader Ginsburg :biohistory:
  82. Proofs and Types by Girard :proof:
    • Proof and Types
  83. Type Theory and Formal Proof: An Introduction :typetheory:proof:
